It adds up: How much do you pay in tampon taxes?


By Priscilla Alabi and Ryan Kelley


Every woman remembers the day she started to menstruate. Whether she is annoyed, horrified or excited by it, menstruation is merely a cyclical part of having a uterus. Yet, women worldwide incur taxes on hygiene products that are definitely not luxury items.

American women in particular spend hundreds of dollars in taxes on their essential feminine hygiene products over the course of a lifetime.

"It’s kind of sick that we tax these specific products," said Catalina Bertani, a student at the SUNY Downstate Midwifery Program. As a woman who devotes her work to women’s healthcare, Bertani said it is "fundamentally unfair that [women] are singled out to be taxed. And low-income women feel it the most."

This same issue inspired Laura Strausfeld of the advocacy organization Period Equity to attend Columbia Law School 26 years ago. "I recall buying tampons and chapstick and noticing that I was taxed on the tampons and not on the chapstick. It was empowering as a law student to be able to look up the law and confirm my suspicion that there was no good reason for chapstick to be exempted from sales tax (on the ostensible ground it has a medical use) and not tampons," she said to her colleagues at Feminist Law Professors.

Strausfeld filed a class action lawsuit against New York state on behalf of five women in March 2016. A few months later in June, New York became one of seven states to actively end the tampon tax. Maryland, Massachusetts, Pennsylvania, Minnesota, New Jersey, Connecticut and Illinois are the others, while Alaska, Delaware, New Hampshire, Oregon and Montana have no sales tax.
